Yesterday was the last day with my cohort this semester, and all I have to say is that I’m filled with so much love for the people in my cohort, the second-year students, and the faculty. Arjun told me at some point this semester to be like wet clay, and let the program and experiences mould me in unfamiliar ways. I let that happen to me, and I’m going to continue to do so. At the end of the first semester, all I have to say is that I’m in so much love.

I’ve had so many conversations during my time, and from them I’ve picked up a lot. It’s fascinating to see the way people talk on the floor, especially when it comes to the process. Emilia and Arjun both explained to me how much fun there is in the process of making things. Both said ‘making things’, and I thought to myself, why did they both describe the process with those specific words? Similarly, Tom, Alanna, and Arjun also refer to the way electricity translates into these experiences as ‘magic’ or ‘magical’. Another common word that I noticed people use, especially in Mimi’s last class, where she gave us a speech and told us more about ‘wonder’. Another such word would be ‘feelings’. These are some words and expressions that stuck with me by the end of the first semester. I was talking to Arjun about why these specific words, and he said they are the simplest, most human-like ways to describe. I thought about it a lot since, and in some ways, the simplicity of these words and the plain language to describe what we do here at ITP is something so special because, in some ways, we are using new media art and technology to articulate/communicate these rather simple/common feelings that we all experience.

At the end of this year, I scoped too big, didn’t have any finished projects, but I’m okay with it. Tom told me that it’s all right that I don’t have any finished projects. Going into the winter show, I knew my project wasn’t complete and that the winter show isn’t the ideal place to exhibit it. So, I used the winter show at the end of this year as a space to get more feedback on how to improve my project, and Tom validated that methodology. Tom also asked me a rather rhetorical question: what is a space outside the web to present this kind of work, where people slow down and interact with a project rather than just quickly browse? Anyway, he said my methodology is a good way to proceed. I told him, that all of my projects at the end of this semester seem like the first version prototypes, and that I want to keep working on them even after presenting, and he said it’s a good way to focus and get out of the program with a few projects, and learn a lot while making and developing those, and I think I’m okay with doing that.

I came into this program, thinking that I would be an animator by the end of it, of some sort. By the end of this semester, I’m more confused than I was before. Again I’m okay with that, I’m learning, exploring, and maybe over winter break or perhaps by the end of next semester, I’ll have some answer, with what I want to get out and be in the real world, but up until then I love learning, so I’m going to continue to do so, with a lot of love.

This was an assignment where we worked with StopMotion Studio for hypercinema. I worked with Bolong and Chuan. This was my first time staying on the floor until 3 am, and my first time, on the way back on the subway, seeing five rats at once, in the Delancey-Essex Station, and waiting twenty minutes for the train. During the project, we all worked so well together and wanted to make it fun, cute, and short story. We used different colored lenses to change the lighting. Bolong had this rabbit/rat at home, and we used it as our character. It was so much fun. In all the other projects that I’ve worked on, most of the team has english as their first language, but this was my first time experiencing a project, where Chuan and Bolong, found a common language to communicate on, so I was first time in a space where I experienced a language barrier and made me understand how difficult it might be for so many people on our floor to communicate. Bolong, sometimes goes by Baron, that’s a name he picked, so it’s easier for native English language speakers to say his name. Chuan called him out during this project. She said that during her undergraduate degree, a professor said that even though it might be difficult for others to say your name, you should force them to say it, because it’s your name and part of your identity, and I agreed with her. Since then, I’ve noticed that Bolong now refers to himself as Bolong, and that’s so sweet. That stuck with me. Also, I love animation, and this project made me feel that, and I felt so connected to this simple assignment.

The GERP group was such a fun experience. I recently found out from Ian that the faculty carefully chose the GERP groups, and I’m so happy I ended up with the one I did. I think we were all equally excited. Our prompt was ‘Playing with boundaries vs. within boundaries,’ and we made a game called ‘Pictobowl’, a playful adaptation of the classic game Fishbowl. The experience made me realise that I love creating games and play experiences, and it was a turning point in the semester in figuring out a new interest of mine: making games.

Then came someone’s birthday. So for this someone, I wrote them something on a postcard for their birthday, and before this, the last time I had ever written to anyone was in school. I never saw myself as a writer in any sense, but after writing a postcard to this person, I understood that I was able to articulate my feelings more clearly through writing and realised that it is a powerful way to express my emotions. So giving this postcard to someone became a pivotal point in learning that I’m a writer, perhaps, and that I love writing.

October was one of the most uncomfortable months. There was a lot of change and adjustment in my personal life, which started affecting how I was creating and working on projects. I would grind my teeth often because of anxiety, which made it really hard to eat on a lot of days. I could tell this month that I’m fundamentally changing as a person, but it was really hard to accept, and in so many ways.

This affected the way I worked on my hypercinema project, specifically because the idea the project sought to translate was so different from what it actually translated. The concept wasn’t developed enough. Presenting that project made me realise the importance of the concept when working on projects, and I realised that a strong concept comes from translating my own experiences and interests into a project. Truly, actually feel with the project that I’m making.

I wanted to try out laser cutting and using the laser-cut pieces to make an animation, and I ended up spending more than 10 hours making 10 seconds of animation. It looks really cool, but it doesn’t convey the story I wanted to tell, so by the end, it was more disappointing than I thought. It taught me a lot about the importance of and ways to come up with ideas.

!['Trash!', physical computation midterm project.](/i-write-sometimes/assets/images/image (9) copy 3.jpg)

By November, I started accepting those changes, and around the same time was my physical computation midterm. I worked with this color sensor, the Adafruit AS7341, and spent a long time trying different approaches and long codes to come up with a solution. The midterm for me, though, was less about the game, ‘Trash!’ itself, but more of an exploration into different sensors. It was truly a fun experience getting stuck in that rabbit hole. I spent many weekends and a lot of time on this midterm. The challenges felt more fun than being a roadblock, and this midterm taught me so much about how to work on a physical computation project. I realised that a physical computation project has code to it, fabrication, and figuring out the electrical components, but the only way you can do the project with enough time in hand is to work on all of the aspects simultaneously.

![Arjun’s ‘Not a Club’ talk on ‘in ITP, why do we do what we do?’](/i-write-sometimes/assets/images/image (9) copy 4.jpg)

Then, during Arjun’s talk on ‘in ITP, why do we do what we do?’, I felt really sad because I felt my dreams were too small. I know that, eventually, in my life, what would truly make me happy is teaching, because during undergrad, I worked as a part-time tutor and, whenever someone was able to understand and benefit from what I was teaching them, I would feel really happy. When they thanked me, it made me feel really proud. Even now, as a graduate assistant, whenever someone from my class comes up to me and shows me their work, I feel so happy. I know that, 20 years down the line, my ideal profession would be to become a professor. I don’t have any desire to live in a big apartment, earn a lot of money, or get famous or anything like that. But during that discussion, that dream felt small, but I thought about it more later, and how that’s actually okay that I don’t need to have crazy big dreams, I could be just happy with something as such, and it doesn’t come from a place where I’m not ambitious, I am a really ambitious and a passionate person, and I feel like the place for that to settle into well would be academia.

My final physical computation was another game I made. It was supposed to be a game about the standard model of particle physics, but when I brought it up in class, a lot of people were confused, so I had to make it much simpler and just help people understand that a proton can be broken down further. After it is broken down, it is made up of three quarks, two up quarks and one down quark. When I was presenting my final, there were many interactions were faulty, but that’s okay, I was able to translate to people that the proton is made up of two up quarks and one down quark, and that made me realise how simplifying the concept, made it much easier for people to get to understand a smaller part of the standard model of particle physics. The project was again really fun to work on. I spent many late nights, but the process made me really happy, and I was able to teach people a difficult concept in particle physics.

![link to the p5.js sketch: https://editor.p5js.org/shlokamohanty/full/QJU0ycKLu](/i-write-sometimes/assets/images/image (10).jpg)

I came up with this idea, a ‘letter writing’ game, that toys with the idea of loving someone for who they are, versus actually loving someone and seeing them for who they are. A lot of the letters people give are more about how the person makes them feel rather than about the person. Given the nature of the project and the time I’ll need, it will be done over winter break and the semester. I thought I’d use the hypercinema final and the introduction to computational media final to prototype it, but I couldn’t because of time constraints and the scope of the prototype itself. However, in the introduction to computational media, we learnt about Markov Chains. With learning about Markov Chains, I wanted to use it and see if I can use the letters I have right now, and see if it can come up with new and different storylines within each letter from the combinations it picks up. It does that to some degree now. It was really fun exploring that, and I think I want to work on it further, too.

Finally, the project I presented for the winter show was the one Ryan and I worked on for our visual journalism final. It was a pair project where I had so much fun working. Ryan and I had divided our work very clearly. He’d work on the code and sound, and I would work on the illustrations and animation. Then we’d come up with the storylines together based on the survey we did. It was so fun learning about what people experience in New York City, how sometimes a transit experience can be so annoying, but sometimes it’s really direct, too, and that’s as simple as the journey it encapsulates. Someone during the winter show told me they wanted to experience a longer storyline, like a longer walk to one of the subways in the experience, but in reality, if the walk is longer, it would get really annoying quickly, which is an interesting thought. This is another unfinished project that needs a lot more work, and during the winter show we got plenty of feedback on how to improve it. Eventually, we want to submit it to places.

What felt really touching was that during the show, someone saw my illustration and asked was I an artist before coming into this program, and I chuckled and said no I studied economics and data science, but it felt really special being called an artist, because I didn’t see myself as one, and I don't necessarily still do.

Conversations with Arjun throughout the semester have been really dear to me because he has taken the time to help me understand and approach the arts. It is really hard to get that out of the classes because they are so technical.

I’ve noticed one word that’s commonly used to describe me now within the cohort, and that’s ‘sweet’, I like that association with who I am, and I am starting to realise that I want to create projects that are about people, for people, and just capturing the experiences that people have, and in some ways I’m filled with so much love and care, that I want my projects in some manner translate that and in even small ways make people feel seen, cared and loved for.

I’ve learnt so much during this semester, and I’ve had so much fun working on all my projects, it makes me so excited for next semester. Even during the much-needed break, I will miss going to the floor and seeing and interacting with people every day until January.
