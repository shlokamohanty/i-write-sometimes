The discussion on energy made me think about my own creative practice in a broader sense, and how I want to change or start looking at it. These are some points that particularly resonated with me during the discussion. 

```who gets to afford different kinds of energy?```

```energy as an invisible infrastructure```

```where does the material come from and how does that affect people?```

```
much more hollistic conversation
-> acknowledging the reality / the depressing part
```

Energy as an invisible structure is something I've thought about a lot, but in a metaphorical manner, how people have struggles, which often go by invisible. I’ve been really into how people make up cities, and these smaller experiences that people go through that make up the larger aspects, and how people are what give cities personalities. Hence, this invisibility, in a metaphoric sense, but energy is also a huge component of a city. 

The question of the materials where they come from, again, conversations are not often had, and it comes from forms of invisible labor. The question of solar panels in terms of decorative practices for buildings continues to raise more questions about who can afford them. In some way, aesthetics are gatekept. But, I liked the conclusion being more holistic conversations and acceptance, might be the best way, and ties in largely with how I want to try and create things. Acknowledge more. and question more. Didn’t really realise design holds so much power.

The discussion made me think that I need to be more empathetic, in a more critical way, not just solve a functional problem, but actively question who is being left out of the solution. Acknowledge the invisible struggles of the people, and sometimes, accept there’s no solution. Look at things, witness more, listen more. Sometimes, the most functional thing is to really stop and look. 

It’s more important to converse and acknowledge.

And comes back to, do I want to tell my stories or be a biographer of some sort? Still don’t know how to tell stories of other people without it taking away their agency. How do you tell stories, without speaking for someone but just witnessing?
