<div class="day-block pivotal">
<span class="day-label">day 7</span>
I realised today that, while drawing a lot of these memories, I’m drawing myself into them. The way I recall my memories is from an observer’s perspective. On reading more about this, I learnt that PTSD causes people to distance themselves from their memories. People with PTSD or trauma can become hyper-focused on their surroundings rather than on themselves, and that’s why they can look at it from a perspective. Didn’t realise how dissociated I was until today, so I cried. As mentioned earlier, before doing this project, I wrote a bunch of letters to people and realised that it was probably my first time writing and recalling experiences from a first-person point of view. I was an observer in all my relationships with people before moving to New York. Scary realisation of how dissociated I was. So, now I’ll add another marker in my code to indicate whether the memories were in first- or third-person point of view. I’ll also make an active effort to recall memories from a first-person perspective.

But I question now whether I should be telling people’s stories. Today’s discussion made me realize that telling other people’s stories is just a transcription. Even though my intentions might be good, fair, and innocent, telling their story still takes away some agency from the other person. So how do I tell stories in a manner that involves the other person without taking away their agency? The other thing is that there’s always going to be a perspective, hence a bias, involved. In some ways, that perspective and bias are what human understanding is all about. But how can I be sure that what I say is told in the most just manner? Is there even a just manner? But it really makes me question how to write. The fact that drawing is a form of transcription, too. These memories are essentially stories told through illustrations, and they’re entirely from my perspective. Makes me evaluate how to do storytelling in general in a manner that’s sensitive, caring, and involved. I am thinking back to my letters. The intent was to make people feel seen in the way I wrote those, but I don’t think I did. They are sweet, but writing about how the person is in your eyes isn’t making them feel seen, particularly. It’s your perspective still. It’s only possible through conversations. I can only make people feel seen by listening intently in conversations, not through one-sided letters, because when I wrote the letters, I just wrote about my experiences and memories in a disguised manner. I never really asked whether the other people ever resonated with that. I try to fix problems immediately, but that’s not making people feel seen, particularly. Making people feel seen is acknowledging their emotions first. It’s asking more questions and being curious about them. I got it all wrong. Because all of that was transcribing. So I have to see people and see myself to be actually able to tell their stories and mine.
